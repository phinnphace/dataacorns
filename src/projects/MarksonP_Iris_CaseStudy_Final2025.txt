The Iris dataset is ubiquitous in introductory statistics and machine learning. It is typically
presented as a parable of clean categorical separation: three distinct species (Iris setosa, I.
versicolor, and I. virginica) perfectly distinguishable by their measurements. However, a
closer examination reveals a more complicated truth. While I. setosa is distinct, I. versicolor
and I. virginica overlap significantly—a nuance explicitly noted in the dataset’s canonical
documentation yet frequently erased in introductory pedagogy. The Iris dataset's
documentation explicitly states that two species overlap (“One class is linearly separable
from the other; the latter are not linearly separable from each other”), yet it serves as the
canonical example of perfect classification in statistics and machine learning education.2
This contradiction is not accidental—it reveals how statistical pedagogy systematically
erases empirical complexity to preserve the myth of clean categorical boundaries.
Science is methodology, but methodology is not neutral. The tools we use to
observe and interpret the world are shaped by the values and assumptions of their
creators. Statistics, in particular, emerged from efforts by a small cohort of white men (non
exhaustive: Galton, Pearson, and Fisher) seeking to validate the differences between white
and black their ideologies insisted upon. Creating a measure would shift opinion into the
domain of objectivity. Creating measures, scales and standards required a “from” or a point
to measure proximity. How else would one know how close one was or was not to the
mark? An idealized white, cisgender male body to quantify human “difference” was the
benchmark. Everybody nonwhite, non-cisgender male has been measure to this. Medicine,
even today still has its benchmarks (within normal limits) as a white cis male body. Oxygen
saturation measuring, kidney function and pain presentation are not remnants of some
past misstep.
What often goes unexamined is how this logic of deviation from the presumed
standard (central tendency) functions as alpha and omega by mirroring and reinforcing
systems of social classification and hierarchy. Although Fisher later distanced himself from
some extreme forms of biological determinism, the statistical frameworks he helped
formalize were nonetheless forged in an era when eugenics, typological thinking, and
colonial science were dominant paradigms 1.

The core research question began simply: Are there significant differences between
the species, and can they be accurately predicted? However, the divergence between one
analysts’ findings conducted through a standard predictive lens (perfect separation) and a
second analysis (structural overlap) reframed the inquiry.
Because the consultant and I worked independently, our workflows accidentally became
a natural experiment in statistical pedagogy. One analysis followed the predictive
pipeline toward separability; the other used an exploratory-diagnostic lens to surface
structural overlap. Their divergence was not a disagreement but the case study itself: a
demonstration of how training shapes what analysts see and what they discard.
Through a critical re-analysis, this report demonstrates how the choice of lens
determines the visibility of the data. When we prioritize algorithmic separation over
diagnostic complexity, we risk creating models that are mathematically accurate but
structurally false—a legacy that extends from Fisher’s methodology to modern debates on
biological determinism in population health, policy and our lives.
A Note on Positionality and Reflexive Methodology
I approach this dataset through a specific critical lens: one that seeks to uncover how
ideological commitments become embedded in seemingly neutral statistical practice. This
lens necessarily guides my methodological choices. Unlike the uncritical application of
statistical procedures, I explicitly surface my theoretical commitments, following
Kincheloe's (2008) notion of "critical transparency." I view the Iris dataset not as a neutral
"Hello World" for machine learning, but as a seed crystal of a particular statistical
ideology—one that prioritizes separation over overlap, purity over mixture, and prediction
over understanding.

III. Results
The investigation revealed a stark contrast between the illusion of clean separation
presented in the consultant's initial report and the nuanced reality uncovered through re
analysis.
3.1 The consultant’s analysis painted a picture of definitive separability, but this conclusion
relied on systemic methodological choices:
The "Significance" of Omission: The consultant ran linear regressions (functionally
equivalent to ANOVA) on three of the four variables: Sepal Length, Petal Length, and Petal
Width. All returned high significance (p < 2e-16). However, the consultant excluded Sepal
Width—the one variable that shows significant overlap between Versicolor and Virginica.
By removing the "messy" data point, the analysis artificially inflated the clarity of the
species boundaries.
The Conflation of Prediction and Inference: While linear regression and ANOVA are
mathematically equivalent (both represent the General Linear Model), the consultant
utilized regression purely as a classification tool rather than a hypothesis test. A rigorous
ANOVA framework asks, "Do specific group means differ?" and mandates assumption checks
and post-hoc analysis. In contrast, the consultant’s approach implicitly asked, "Can I
separate these groups?" By prioritizing separation over inference, the analysis treated the
biological overlap in Sepal Width not as a diagnostic finding, but as "noise" to be discarded
in pursuit of a cleaner, albeit less accurate, model.
Circular PCA Logic: The consultant performed a regression using the first Principal
Component (PC1) as the dependent variable against Species. Since PC1 is mathematically
constructed to maximize variance (which is driven by species differences), this test was
circular—it could only show that the species were different because the math defined them

The Broken Classifier: The consultant presented a multinomial logistic regression with a
"perfect" confusion matrix for the training set. However, a review of the raw code revealed
that the model threw multiple warnings: fitted probabilities numerically 0 or 1 occurred and
the algorithm did not converge. These warnings indicate "complete separation"—the model
failed to converge because Setosa is too distinct, while the other classes overlapped. Rather
than addressing this breakdown, the warnings were suppressed, and the "results" were
presented as valid.
Post-hoc tests would have revealed that versicolor/virginica do not significantly differ on
sepal width—a direct contradiction to the 'clean separation' narrative. The system requires
analysts to stop at p < 0.001 because deeper interrogation exposes the dataset's explicit
warning about overlap. This isn't incompetence; it's the logical endpoint of teaching stats as
'find significance, then stop.' In population health, this produces race-based models that

IV. Conclusion: Methodology as Ideology
In population health, this produces race-based models that "predict" disparities
while ignoring structural drivers. By asking "can I separate groups?" (classification) rather
than "why do these groups differ?" (inference), analysts encourage the use of race as a
biological proxy.
We see this logic fail in high-stakes contexts, such as using race to predict infant mortality
while ignoring social determinants, and in everyday policy, such as the debate in Wisconsin
over when elders should stop shoveling snow. This is not merely a statistical shortcut; it is
the very bedrock of biological determinism/essentialism.
By treating Age as a binary cutoff for "shoveling ability," we ignore functional health. By
treating race as a binary cutoff for health risk, we ignore racism. In both cases, the model
creates a dangerous fiction: it reifies the category (age/race) as the cause, effectively hiding
the actual mechanism (health/racism). This protects the system from scrutiny while
pathologizing the individuals within it.
It is critical to acknowledge that the consultant’s approach was not technically
'incorrect' within the paradigm of standard data science training; rather, it was optimized
for a different objective. The consultant’s methodology prioritized parsimony and
separation—the hallmarks of efficient predictive modeling. By this metric, the removal of
'noisy' variables like Sepal Width is a feature, not a bug, as it creates a cleaner, more
definitive classification boundary 9.
However, the client’s objective as an equity-centered scientist was not classification,
but diagnosis and structural understanding. Through this lens, the 'noise' of overlapping
groups is not waste to be discarded, but the primary signal of biological (or social) reality.
The friction in this collaboration did not arise from errors, but from the distinct lineages of
STEM training we came from: one shaped by the predictive logic of statistics, the other
shaped by the structural and contextual demands of population health. Both approaches

were internally coherent; their divergence simply revealed how training directs attention
toward different forms of truth. The consultant succeeded in building a map that was easy

to run code, but to recognize when a proxy is masking a mechanism11. We must learn to
retain the "Sepal Widths"—the messy, overlapping variables—because that is often where
the structural truth resides.
