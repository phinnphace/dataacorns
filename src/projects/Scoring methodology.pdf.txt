The road density variable, PROP_PRIM_SEC_ROADS (Proportion of Primary and Secondary 
Roads), serves as a fundamental measure of the built environment's capacity to support 
mobility, often acting as a proxy for walkability or vehicular efficiency. 
Aspect      Rationale 
Mobility 
Proxy 
In rural and micropolitan areas like Decatur County, this variable 
indicates the quality and density of the network needed for driving, 
cycling, or walking—especially where transit is absent. Low density 
means poor infrastructure and reduced mobility. 
Walkability 
Proxy 
High density of primary/secondary roads generally suggests a more 
interconnected, grid-like network, which correlates with better 
walkability than low-density, sprawling, or disconnected road 
systems. 
Vulnerability 
Direction 
For this component, low road density is treated as high vulnerability. 
If there are fewer primary/secondary roads, access is inherently 
more difficult.

### Standardization via Z-Scores 
 
This model standardizes component variables using Z-scores to place heterogeneous 
measures on a common scale prior to aggregation. This choice is pragmatic and 
problematic. I do not pretend that it is not "inferential" because it carries several explicit 
assumptions and limitations. I don't like it either and would very much like an alternative.  
 
Z-score standardization assumes that variables are meaningfully centered around a global 
mean and scaled by a global standard deviation. In classical applications, this approach is 
often paired with assumptions of approximate normality, independence of observations, 
and large-sample behavior (via the Central Limit Theorem). These conditions are **not 
fully met** in this context. 
 
First, the component variables are not assumed to be independent. Transportation access, 
economic conditions, infrastructure, and connectivity are structurally entangled by design. 
Z-scores are used here to standardize magnitude, not to imply orthogonality or causal 
separability among components. 
 
Second, normality is not assumed. Several variables exhibit skewed or bounded 
distributions, particularly in rural or micropolitan contexts. Z-scores are employed as a 
relative scaling mechanism rather than as a probabilistic model of the data-generating 
process. 
 
Third, this analysis does not rely on asymptotic inference. The use of Z-scores is not 
intended to support hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, or population-level 
----------------Page (6) Break----------------
generalization. Instead, standardization enables within-model comparison of relative 
position and contribution across components. 
 
To mitigate small-sample distortion, all Z-scores are calculated using global reference 
distributions rather than tract-level or county-level means. This anchors local observations 
to a broader structural context and avoids artificially inflating variance in small geographic 
samples. 
 
In this model, Z-scores function as a **comparability device**, not a claim about statistical 
independence, normality, or causal structure. Their use is justified insofar as the model’s 
purpose is exploratory, comparative, and diagnostic rather than predictive or inferential. 
 
### Assumption: Completeness and Accuracy of Grocery Store Data 
 
 
This model assumes that grocery store locations drawn from publicly available datasets 
represent a sufficiently complete approximation of the food retail landscape for the 
purposes of structural comparison. Store presence is treated as binary and exhaustive: a 
store either exists in the dataset or it does not. 
 
This assumption is knowingly fragile. While multiple data sources were cross-checked, the 
model does not independently verify store operational status, food quality, affordability, 
cultural relevance, or temporal stability. Stores may be missing, misclassified, closed, 
inaccessible, or functionally unusable for reasons not captured in spatial data. 
 
Crucially, this uncertainty is **not corrected for within the scoring framework**. No 
weighting, penalty, or confidence adjustment is applied to account for incomplete or 
inaccurate store representation. As a result, geographic density and availability signals 
should be interpreted as provisional structural indicators rather than confirmed access 
conditions. 
 
This assumption reflects a broader constraint of food access research: the absence of 
reliable, continuously updated ground-truth data on food availability. The model treats 
grocery access as “present and accounted for” only in the narrow sense that locations are 
mapped, not in the substantive sense that food is accessible, affordable, or usable.
